2D Rigging
My first introduction to rigging was actually something I wanted to try out for fun, creating a 2D model that could be rigged to to express itself based on your input facial paramaters, commonly known as V-Tubing. Although I never really did anything with the models I made, I kept iterating with all new versions as I simply enjoyed creating them.
How it Works
A 2D Model, made using the software live2D Cubism, is a single character created by using multiple layers as individual pieces that make up the model. Each of these layers is assigned certain positions and deformation states at values for multiple keyframes.
For example, each layer that makes up an eye has keyframes assigned for x, y, and z movement, as well as eyeOpen and eyeSmile (plus some other ones used in special toggles and keybinds). The xyz parameters adjust the position of each item, while the other keyframes adjust deformation that occours on the image. In one eye, each layer needs unique keyframes in order to properly warp to achieve the desired shapes.
While a very simple concept, actual execution ends up taking a very long time and a very good understanding of the space that you're imagining your character in, as there isn't actually any 3D depth to them. Making and rigging a model requires a lot of planning and design to make it right, and it was especially true in my case. as you'll see later, I ended up creating a lot of designs for the tech of my model from scratch.
Versions
Almost as soon as I had finished my first model, I had thought a lot about different ways I could rig more depth, range, and expression into it. With those ideas in mind, I ended up redesigning, redrawing, and rerigging whole new versions of my little raccoon friend (who would later be named Cray). Each shift in technology dubbed a new version for the model. The final versions of each model are below, at versions 3.2, 7.3, and (in progress) 8.1.
Version 3.2
With the initial version, I learned the essential basics of the software, and didn't do anything too crazy. These tools ended up being in all the other versions to come, so I'll describe them out here.
Basic Movement and Dimensionality
The basic movement of a model is controlled by the position of your head: left and right (x movement), up and down (y movement), and tilt rotation (z movement). Other controls include things like how open eyes are, what the mouth looks like, position of eyebrows, etc.
Because of the nature of the human (or in this case not-human) face, rigging a proper model requires knowledge of and planning for multiple of those parameters to be changing at once. As well as that, deformation order (deformation, rotation, scale, translation) is also incredibly important to understand and use to your advantage to make sure that the model deforms properly.
Physics!
The extra (but honestly incredibly critical) bit that gives the model way more life is the automatic secondary movement of the model, also known as physics. In this model, I implemented physics on the ears, side flufts, tail, and tassles of Cray.
Physics in this program revolve around a system that uses a pendulum. This gives the parameters physics are attatched to that delayed motion and sway that comes after. However, unlike 3D engine physics, this one works by alternating along a slider of set values- meaning the rigger in this scenario has to create keyframes for the pendulum to vary between.
Toggles
Another aspect of models is toggles. These are essentially additional keyframes that usually rest at 0, and are activated with a non "automatic" input parameter like a manual keybind.
While the software now supports creating these files inside of the application that controls them, at the time of making this model these expression files had to be created before importing the model into the program. Creating one of those files involves specifying set values for keyframes at the time the toggle is activated, and how long it takes for them to activate.
Version 2
With the second version, I looked to expand the range of motion of the character. Additional layers and image culling was used to change the shape of the head so drastically with large movement. In addition to that, controls for ear movement were attatched to eyebrow movement for ease of control.
Version 3 (in development)
The current version is in development right now, with physics and other movement addons are currently being added. The entire facial system was restructured, with image shapes and their outlines being separated in order to keep a more consistent outline and shape on turns and deformation.

3D Rigging
I am somewhat new to 3D rigging, but I've found that my prior experience with 2D rigging has been incredibly helpful in both visualizing the things I need to create my model to do, and actually creating the model. For the fall 2022 project I did, I created the models for the main characters: 2 bipeds, and one octopus monster.
Bipedal Main Character
I modeled and rigged the main character for the animation over the course of about one to two weeks. In that time, I sculpted, rigged and weighted, and created blend shape expressions for myself and my teammates to use in animation.
Some of the models features include: Eyes controlled with a driven key attatched to the UV maps of eye planes, IK spring solver legs, curve and cluster controlled tail, as well as structure for animating individual finger curl and ear movement. Despite the somewhat stubby appendages of the character, I was able to make the model capture a pretty wide range of motion.
Monstrous Antagonist
The antagonist of our movie was really where I had the most fun in creating a villain. Naturally, given our limited experience and even more limited amount of time to put our plan together, I had to come up with a control scheme for a monster that would allow us to maximize his presence. What I came up with was a central body piece controlled by a curve, a set of three tentacles that would remain around him, and a theoretically infinite number of detatched motion pathed tentacles that could be aimed and placed wherever based on the position of the camera, and adjusted to time.
Part of the model also included blend shapes for eyes and mouth open/close, as well as a high-emission plane located in the body to give him the contrasting black and white appearance we wanted.
